Section 1

What a potential client sees right now

If someone searches for Goodfellows Epsom and clicks through, here is what they find:

Website — as seen today

Index of /

A plain directory listing. No firm name, no content, no way to make contact.

The server is live but nothing has been published. To most visitors this looks broken, or as though the firm is not open.

This does not reflect the experience or reputation of an established firm. A first-time visitor would have no way of knowing that.

  • No firm name, description, or history visible
  • No services or areas of law listed
  • No phone number, email, or address
  • No indication the firm is open or established
  • Nothing that reflects the firm's existing reputation

Section 2

Why this matters

In practice, people rarely look at just one firm. They open several at once, compare reviews, location and website clarity, then decide.

If one option is clearer and easier to act on, the decision is made quickly.

01

Under 30 seconds from first landing, most people have already formed a view. If the site gives no useful signal, they move on.

02

The comparison includes reviews, location and how easy it is to take the next step. The website is part of that.

03

This is less about capability and more about whether that capability is visible when someone is deciding who to contact.

Section 3

What good looks like at this stage

The standard is not high. A clean, working website with the right information in order is enough.

  • Clear services. What you do, in plain language.
  • Immediate trust. Who you are, established since 2014, SRA regulated, visible client feedback.
  • Simple contact. Phone, email and address easy to find.
  • Mobile-friendly. Most visitors will arrive on a phone.
  • Fast and accessible. Loads quickly, works for everyone.
  • Local. Rooted in Epsom, serving the area.

Platforms like Google and ReviewSolicitors already signal the firm's reputation. The website should reinforce that, not contradict it.

Section 4

Recommended first steps

  1. Get something live. A single well-structured page is far better than what is there now. It does not need to be perfect.
  2. Structure content clearly. Services, history, who you are, how to get in touch. Plain English throughout.
  3. Add a simple enquiry flow. A contact form so people can reach you without hunting for a number.
  4. Align with what already exists. Done well, the website reflects and reinforces the reputation that already exists elsewhere.
