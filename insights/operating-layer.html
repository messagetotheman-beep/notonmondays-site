Operations · Digital Delivery

You don't need a full digital team
you need the right operating layer

The problem is rarely talent. It is the absence of the layer that connects what leadership decides to what teams actually deliver.

April 2026 · 7 min read · Operations · Digital Delivery

On this page

  • What goes wrong
  • The missing operating layer
  • What the operating layer is
  • What good looks like
  • Why hiring more people is not the answer

TL;DR

  • The slowdown in most digital work is not a skills problem. It is a coordination problem.
  • Between setting direction and delivering it, there is a gap most organisations have never deliberately closed.
  • The operating layer is not a team or a programme. It is the combination of accountabilities, rhythms, and standards that make work move reliably.
  • Adding headcount without it usually makes things slower, not faster.

The board has agreed the priorities. The teams are capable. The tools are in place.

And yet progress is slower than anyone expected.

Work is happening, but decisions are queuing at the top. Blockers are not surfacing until they have become expensive. Delivery is carrying on the shoulders of a small number of people who are holding too much.

This is not a talent problem. The people are good. The issue is that the organisation has not built the layer that lets good people work without constantly compensating for structural gaps.

What goes wrong

The pattern is remarkably consistent across organisations of different sizes and sectors.

  • Someone owns the vision and someone owns the technology. Nobody owns the connective tissue between the two.
  • Decisions that should take an afternoon wait weeks because only senior people have the authority to make them.
  • Teams work in parallel but are not genuinely aligned. Assumptions diverge quietly until they collide.
  • Blockers get raised in standups and noted in a spreadsheet. They rarely reach the person who could actually resolve them.
  • The organisation depends on two or three people who know how everything connects. When one of them is unavailable, things stall.
  • Every retrospective surfaces the same problems. Nothing structurally changes.

None of these are failures of effort or intention. They are failures of structure. And they are fixable.

The missing operating layer

Most organisations can name what they want to achieve. Most delivery teams are doing real work. The breakdown is in the space between those two things.

That space needs something that can translate direction into work, make decisions within agreed boundaries, hold quality, and maintain momentum when things get complicated. In most organisations, that space is either empty or occupied by whoever happens to be paying closest attention that week.

When it is empty, work accumulates at the top because only senior people can make the calls. Or it drifts at the bottom because teams are waiting for guidance that never quite arrives in a form they can act on.

The result is an organisation where capable people are working hard but the system is not working well.

Key point

The operating layer is not a gap in your org chart. It is a gap in how your organisation makes decisions and moves work forward.

The symptom

Everyone is busy and nothing is moving

Work is happening across multiple teams and tools. But nobody can say with confidence what is on track, what is blocked, or what is coming next.

The symptom

The same decisions go to the same people

Senior leaders are making calls that should sit lower. Not because they want to. Because there is no clarity about who else is authorised to make them.

The symptom

Each retrospective ends with the same list

The problems are familiar. The actions get noted. By the next review, the same issues are back. The environment that created them has not changed.

What the operating layer is

It is not a digital team. It is not a transformation programme. It is not a new hire.

The operating layer is the combination of things that let work move reliably without depending on heroics or constant senior attention. Most organisations already have the building blocks. What is usually missing is the deliberate assembly of them.

  • A clear mandate at each level, so people know what they can decide and what needs escalating
  • A regular rhythm of reviews that surfaces problems while there is still time to act on them
  • Shared standards that reduce the negotiation cost of coordinating across teams
  • A way of making work visible that is useful to the people doing it, not just to leadership

None of this requires a large programme. It requires someone to look honestly at how work actually moves through the organisation and to close the gaps deliberately.

The goal is not to add process. It is to remove the friction that is slowing things down and making people's jobs harder than they need to be.

What good looks like

Decisions land at the right level

Senior people are not being pulled into operational calls that should sit lower. Delivery teams are not waiting for permission to make decisions they are qualified to make. Each level has a clear mandate and uses it.

Blockers surface before they become expensive

Problems reach the right people quickly, while there is still room to do something about them. Not in a retrospective three weeks later when the cost has already compounded.

Coordination is predictable, not heroic

The organisation does not run on the goodwill of a few people who are willing to hold everything together. The operating layer is a system. When those people are on leave or move on, work continues at pace.

New people can plug in without a tour guide

Context is not locked inside individual heads. Someone new can understand how decisions get made, where to raise problems, and what good work looks like without needing weeks of informal onboarding.

Without it

Work moves, but only just

Progress depends on who is available, who has context, and who is willing to absorb coordination work that nobody has formally assigned. When those people are stretched, everything slows.

The shift

Structure replaces heroics

Accountabilities are clear. Reviews are regular and useful. Problems reach the right people quickly. Work moves because the system supports it, not because individuals are carrying it.

With it in place

Teams move with more confidence

Decisions get made at the right level. Senior time goes to the right things. Blockers surface early. New people can get up to speed without needing the tribal knowledge that usually lives in two or three heads.

Why hiring more people is not the answer

When digital work is stalling, the first instinct is usually to hire. A digital director. A transformation lead. A product manager. Someone whose job it is to fix this.

Sometimes that is the right call. But a new hire walks into an environment where the problems that justified the role are still structurally in place. They are capable. Capable people do not fix structural problems. They work around them.

A digital director who spends their first year in meetings trying to understand who owns what. A transformation lead who cannot get decisions made because the mandate is unclear. A product manager with the skills but not the authority to change how the organisation actually works.

More people does not reduce coordination cost. It increases it. Every additional person is another node in the network. Without the operating layer, that means more alignment conversations, more competing interpretations of priority, more handoffs where context gets lost.

The operating layer creates the conditions for good people to do good work. Without it, you are asking individuals to compensate for a structural gap. Some of them will manage it for a while. None of them should have to.

Signs you might be filling the wrong gap +
  • You have hired a digital or transformation lead but the same problems are still there six months later
  • New hires spend their first weeks learning the workarounds rather than doing the work
  • The same escalations keep reaching the same senior people
  • Nobody can give a clear account of what is on track and what is at risk
  • Delivery depends on one or two people. Everyone knows it. Nobody has changed it.
