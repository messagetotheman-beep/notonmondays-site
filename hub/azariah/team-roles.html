Roles & Ownership

Who we are, what we own, and how we move as one team.

Functions · Owners · Gaps

Context

Why this exists

As Azariah grows, it matters that everyone, including Jok, Jeff, and the wider team, knows who owns what. Clarity of roles is not bureaucracy. It is the thing that lets a small, capable team move fast without creating chaos.

Clarity of roles = clarity of ownership = speed without chaos.

This document sets out six core functions the Azariah needs covered. These are not job titles. They are areas of ownership. Right now, some are clearly assigned. Others are shared. And a few are gaps we still need to close.

The Functions

Six areas, one team

Every function below must have a clear owner: one person. If ownership is unclear, the function will drift. These are not about seniority. They are about accountability.

01

Direction & Strategy

"Where are we going, and why?"

This is the work of deciding what Azariah is building toward and who it is for. It means holding the vision for how we position ourselves within healthcare systems, maintaining the relationships that matter most, and making the calls that keep everything aligned.

  • Vision and positioning, including NHS alignment
  • Strategic partnerships and investor relationships
  • Final decision-making authority

02

Delivery & Operations

"Are we actually delivering value week to week?"

This is the engine room. Someone needs to own the rhythm of the team — making sure work flows, blockers are removed, and that what gets planned actually gets done. Without clear ownership here, everything slows down or falls between the cracks.

  • Sprint planning, workflow, and Jira
  • Team coordination and blocker removal
  • Making sure commitments are met

03

Product & Service Design

"Are we building the right thing?"

Health products fail when they are built for the system rather than the people inside it. This function owns the problem — understanding what clinicians and patients actually need, shaping what we build around that, and making sure everything holds up in an NHS context.

  • Understanding clinical and patient needs
  • Defining the problems worth solving
  • NHS usability and service design

04

Technology & Engineering

"Can we build it, and will it last?"

Someone needs to own the technical direction of the product. Not just writing code, but making the right architectural decisions, setting standards, and ensuring what we build today does not become a burden tomorrow.

  • Architecture and technical standards
  • Feature delivery and development quality
  • Managing developers and technical debt

05

Growth, Proposals & Partnerships

"How does work come in?"

This function turns Azariah's credibility into pipeline. It covers how we position ourselves in bids and proposals, how we build strategic partnerships, and how we make our commercial pipeline repeatable rather than opportunistic.

  • Proposals, bids, and positioning
  • Decks, outreach, and pipeline management
  • Strategic partner development

06

Systems & Enablement

"Do we have what we need to function well?"

A small team that runs on unclear systems is a slow team. This function owns the tools and infrastructure that let everyone else do their best work — from Slack and Jira to the CRM, the website, and the visibility that tells us whether things are working.

  • Slack, Jira, CRM, LinkedIn, and website
  • Internal automation and data visibility
  • Keeping the operation running smoothly

What This Means

The things to watch

The team is more complete than it might appear. Most functions have coverage. What remains is about depth, clarity, and making sure no one person becomes a bottleneck as things scale.

Watch: Jeff's load across multiple functions.
Jeff is currently active across Delivery, Product ownership, Growth support, and Systems. David's fractional support and Rico's project management help significantly — but as Azariah grows, this needs deliberate redistribution. The goal is for Jeff to own one function deeply, not five functions lightly.

Watch: Product ownership as a shared responsibility.
Having Jok and Jeff both acting as product owners works for now, but it can create ambiguity over decisions. As the product matures, a single accountable product lead, even part-time or fractional, will help the team move with more confidence.

Quick win: Name the designer.
Design is covered, but the role is unnamed in the team's shared understanding. Formalising this by simply adding the name to the ownership map closes a small but real gap in how the team sees itself.

Immediate Actions

Three things to do now

  1. Confirm and publish the ownership map

    The table on the previous page is close to complete. Fill in the designer's name, confirm owners with each person, and pin it in Slack. Add it to the Azariah Playbook. Reference it in onboarding. This one action removes most of the remaining ambiguity.

  2. Have an honest conversation about Jeff's load

    Not as a problem — as a planning exercise. Map out what Jeff currently owns, identify what can move to Rico, David, or others, and agree on what Jeff's primary function actually is. This protects both the person and the operation.

  3. Start planning for a dedicated product lead

    Joint product ownership between Jok and Jeff is workable short-term. But defining what that role looks like and who could fill it, whether internally or fractionally, means you are ready to move quickly when the time comes.
