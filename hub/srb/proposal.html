Context

The bigger picture we noticed.

The individual findings above matter. But the more important observation is structural: the digital estate of most professional services firms tends to be managed reactively and in silos. Website here. Social there. SEO when something goes wrong. Reviews when someone complains.

The firms that win locally over the next five years will be the ones who treat their digital presence as a joined-up system, one that reflects their values, serves their clients, and compounds over time. That's the conversation we think is worth having.

Discovery happens online, decisions are made offline
The majority of clients searching for a solicitor in your area will encounter SRB digitally before they ever speak to anyone. That first impression shapes whether they pick up the phone at all.
Your clients have specific accessibility needs
Wills, probate, lasting powers of attorney, clinical negligence. Your practice areas disproportionately serve elderly and vulnerable individuals. A site that fails accessibility standards is a barrier between those clients and the help they need.
Mobile is where most people will find you first
Across legal services in the UK, mobile now accounts for over 60% of web traffic. A site that isn't optimised for mobile is losing potential clients before the page has even finished loading.
Google Reviews are the new word of mouth
SRB's reputation is built on decades of client relationships. That trust exists, but it isn't yet visible online in the way it could be. A structured, sensitive approach to gathering reviews could transform your local search presence within months.
Nearly fifty years deserves to be told properly
The heritage, the depth of expertise, the local roots. These are real differentiators. But they only work if they're communicated in language that clients actually understand and trust. "Over 35 years" doesn't do justice to what SRB has built.
Silos are the enemy of a coherent client experience
When SEO, brand, accessibility, content, and social are managed separately and reactively, the result is inconsistency. Clients sense it even when they can't name it. A joined-up strategy produces a compounding effect that reactive maintenance never can.

One thing worth saying early: we don't just assess and advise. Where the work calls for it — and for SRB, the website clearly does — we design and build. You wouldn't be bringing in another agency further down the line. That's us too.

Questions worth sitting with.

We're not here to tell you what to do. But we've found that the most useful thing a strategic partner can offer, early on, is the right questions. These are ours.

01
When a potential client Googles "solicitor Twickenham" at 9pm on a Sunday, what do they find, and what do they do next?
The answer to this question shapes your entire acquisition funnel. Most firms have never tested it from the outside.
02
How does a client who doesn't know which solicitor they need find the right person on your website?
Most legal websites are organised around the firm's structure, not the client's question. The two are rarely the same.
03
When a client completes a matter with SRB, what happens to that relationship digitally?
Referrals and reviews are the most powerful growth levers available to a firm of your standing. Is there a structured approach to cultivating them?
04
If an elderly client with partial sight visited your website today, what would their experience be?
WCAG 2.1 AA compliance is a legal accessibility standard, not a technical nicety. For the clients you serve, it's a matter of inclusion.
05
What does your digital presence say about SRB to someone who's never heard of you?
The brand built over five decades in the community is real. The question is whether your online presence reflects it, or undersells it.
06
Who owns your digital strategy, and do they have the time and resource to drive it proactively?
In most professional services firms, digital sits across several people's desks. The result is reactive maintenance rather than a coherent plan.
